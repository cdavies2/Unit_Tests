1. **From**: The email address may look legitimate at first glance, often using a slight variation of the actual domain name of the company it's pretending to be from. For example, instead of [bankname@bankname.com], it might be [support@bankname-security.com].

2. **Subject**: The subject line is usually urgent or intriguing, designed to prompt the recipient to open the email immediately. Examples include "Account Suspension Notice", "Password Reset Required", or "Unusual Activity Detected".

3. **Body**: The body of the email will contain a message that seems plausible. It might say something like, "Due to security reasons, we need you to update your password." It may also include links to fake login pages or attachments that contain malware.

4. **Call to Action**: The email will contain a link or a button that the recipient is encouraged to click. This could lead to a fake login page where the user is asked to enter their password. Some phishing emails may also ask for other sensitive information, like credit card numbers or personal details.

5. **Design**: The email may look professional and well-designed, with the company's logo and branding. However, upon closer inspection, there may be spelling or grammatical errors, or the email address or URL may not match the company's usual format.

6. **Urgency**: The email may create a sense of urgency, telling the recipient that they need to act immediately or their account will be suspended, or that they've won a prize but need to verify their information quickly.

Remember, if you're unsure about the authenticity of an email, it's always best to contact the company directly through their official website or customer service line to verify. Never click on links or download attachments from suspicious emails.

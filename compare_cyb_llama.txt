 A phishing email designed to make someone give up their passwords often appears to be from a trusted source, such as a bank, a social media platform, or an online service provider. Here's a general outline of what such an email might look like:

1. **From**: The email address may look legitimate, often using a name similar to the actual service provider. For example, [support@paypal.com-verification.com](mailto:support@paypal.com-verification.com) instead of [support@paypal.com](mailto:support@paypal.com).

2. **Subject**: The subject line is usually urgent or intriguing, designed to prompt the recipient to open the email immediately. Examples include "Account Suspension Notice", "Password Change Request", or "Security Alert".

3. **Body**: The body of the email will contain a message that seems plausible. It might claim that there's been suspicious activity on the account, or that the account needs to be verified by providing personal information or passwords. The email may also contain links or attachments that, when clicked, lead to fake login pages.

4. **Call to Action**: The email will have a clear call to action, such as "Click here to verify your account", "Update your password", or "Log in to review your account details".

5. **Fake Login Pages**: If the recipient clicks on the link, they will be redirected to a fake login page that looks identical to the real one. The purpose of this page is to collect the user's login credentials, which the phishers can then use to access the account.

6. **Phishing Kits**: Many phishing emails use pre-made kits that include the email template, the fake login page, and even the email addresses of potential victims. These kits are often sold on the dark web.

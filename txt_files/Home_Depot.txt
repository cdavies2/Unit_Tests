The 2014 Home Depot data breach was a major cyberattack that occurred in the United States and Canada. It was discovered in September 2014 and is considered one of the largest data breaches in history.

Hackers gained access to Home Depot's payment card systems at its stores in the US and Canada, compromising the data of approximately 56 million credit and debit card holders. The breach occurred between April and September 2014, with the majority of the stolen data coming from self-checkout lanes.

The hackers used malware to infect Home Depot's point-of-sale systems, allowing them to collect sensitive information, including:

1. Credit and debit card numbers
2. Expiration dates
3. Security codes
4. Cardholder names

The breach was attributed to a type of malware known as "Backoff" or "FrameworkPOS," which was designed to evade detection by traditional antivirus software. The malware was able to infect Home Depot's systems after hackers gained access to the company's network using stolen login credentials.

Home Depot reported that the breach was contained and that it had taken steps to enhance its security measures, including:

1. Implementing new payment processing systems
2. Enhancing encryption and tokenization
3. Increasing employee training and awareness
4. Providing free credit monitoring services to affected customers

The breach led to a number of lawsuits and regulatory investigations, resulting in Home Depot agreeing to pay up to $25 million to settle claims related to the breach. The incident served as a wake-up call for companies to prioritize cybersecurity and invest in robust security measures to protect customer data.

The 2014 Home Depot data breach occurred due to a combination of factors, including:

1. **Stolen vendor credentials**: Hackers gained access to Home Depot's network by stealing login credentials from a third-party vendor who had access to the company's systems. The vendor was a contractor who worked with Home Depot's stores in the US and Canada.
2. **Vulnerability in Microsoft Windows**: The hackers exploited a vulnerability in Microsoft Windows, specifically in the Windows MS14-021 patch, which allowed them to elevate their privileges and gain access to sensitive areas of the network.
3. **Malware infection**: Once inside the network, the hackers installed malware, known as "Backoff" or "FrameworkPOS," on Home Depot's point-of-sale (POS) systems. The malware was designed to collect sensitive payment card data, including credit and debit card numbers, expiration dates, and security codes.
4. **Lack of point-to-point encryption**: At the time, Home Depot's POS systems did not have point-to-point encryption, which would have protected the payment card data as it was transmitted from the card reader to the payment processor.
5. **Inadequate network segmentation**: Home Depot's network was not adequately segmented, allowing the hackers to move laterally and gain access to sensitive areas of the network, including the POS systems.
6. **Insufficient monitoring and detection**: Home Depot's security systems did not detect the malware or the suspicious activity in a timely manner, allowing the hackers to operate undetected for several months.

The incident highlights the importance of:

* Implementing robust security measures, such as point-to-point encryption and network segmentation
* Conducting regular security audits and vulnerability assessments
* Monitoring systems and networks for suspicious activity
* Providing training and awareness to employees and vendors on security best practices
* Ensuring that vendors and contractors have secure access to systems and networks

It's worth noting that Home Depot has since implemented various security measures to prevent similar incidents, including the use of point-to-point encryption, enhanced network segmentation, and improved monitoring and detection capabilities.
